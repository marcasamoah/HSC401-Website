Sympathetic Signalling

Opposite from the parasympathetic division, sympathetic preganglionic neurons are typically shorter (with the exception of non-chain ganglia) as they do not need to travel far to synapse at the chain ganglia. Postganglionic sympathetic neurons need to be longer to reach their target organs.

All preganglionic sympathetic neurons release ACh, with most targeting the NAChRs of a postganglionic neuron. The binding of ACh causes an EPSP within the postganglionic neuron, in turn causing an action potential, that usually ellicits the release of norepinephrine. This norepinephrine will target adrenergic receptors for a variety of purposes.

The adrenal medulla is special case in that it is supplied by a "preganglionic" neuron that does not synapse with a traditional postganglionic neuron. Instead, the adrenal medulla acts as the postganglionic neuron, containing NAChRs that are activated by the neuron supplying it. This causes an action potential within the adrenal medulla cells that result in the release of epinephrine in the bloodstream. The epinephrine will then target any adrenergic receptors it comes across in the body. Similar to MAChRs, adrenergic receptors are g-protein coupled receptors that modify the activity of a organ in a number of different ways. These will also be explored in the application section.
