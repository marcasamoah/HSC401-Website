Parasympathetic Signalling

Parasympathetic preganganglionic neurons are quite long as they need to be able to reach their respective ganglia which, if you remember, are located close to or inside target organs. For this same reason, parasympathetic postganglionic neurons are much shorter as they do not need to travel as far.

Both preganglionic and postganglionic neurons use the neurotransmitter acetylcholine (ACh) for signalling, but for different functions. The ACh released by preganglionic neurons target nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (NAChRs) located on the postganglionic neuron. Upon the binding of ACh, these receptors open to allow the influx of positively charged sodium ions inside the postganglionic neuron. This is called an excitatory postsynaptic potential (EPSP). The EPSP depolarizes this neuron, jumpstarting an action potential that results in the release of ACh. This ACh will bind to muscarinic acetylcholine receptors (MAChRs) to ellicit a response from the target organ. MAChRs are g-protein coupled receptors that can exert many different effects on an organ when stimulated. These effects will be explored in the application section.
